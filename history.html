20 History Facts

← Back to Home

1. Cleopatra VII lived closer in time to the Moon landing than to the construction of the Great Pyramid of Giza.
2. The Roman Empire at its peak covered around 5 million square kilometres and housed roughly one fifth of the world's population.
3. The Black Death in the 14th century killed an estimated 30–60% of Europe's population, reshaping its economy and society.
4. Oxford University is older than the Aztec Empire — teaching began there around 1096, centuries before Tenochtitlan was founded.
5. The Great Wall of China was built over many centuries by different dynasties and is not a single continuous structure.
6. Viking explorers reached North America around 500 years before Christopher Columbus made his 1492 voyage.
7. The Mongol Empire was the largest contiguous land empire in history, stretching from the Pacific to Eastern Europe.
8. Napoleon Bonaparte was not unusually short — his reputation came partly from a misunderstanding of French and English measurement units.
9. The Library of Alexandria was one of the largest repositories of knowledge in the ancient world, and its loss has been debated for centuries.
10. Ancient Egyptians used a 365-day calendar and were among the first civilizations to develop a standardized writing system.
11. The Silk Road connected China to the Mediterranean for over a millennium, spreading goods, religions, and ideas across continents.
12. World War I was partly triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo in June 1914.
13. The printing press, invented by Gutenberg around 1440, revolutionized the spread of information across Europe.
14. Ancient Rome had a complex system of roads, aqueducts, and public buildings that influenced infrastructure for centuries.
15. The Industrial Revolution began in Britain in the late 18th century and transformed manufacturing, transport, and daily life.
16. Genghis Khan united the Mongol tribes and created the foundation for one of history's most feared military forces.
17. The French Revolution beginning in 1789 reshaped ideas about democracy, human rights, and the role of monarchy in government.
18. Ancient Greece gave rise to democracy, philosophy, the Olympic Games, and foundational works of Western literature.
19. The Cold War between the United States and Soviet Union lasted from the late 1940s until the dissolution of the USSR in 1991.
20. Hieroglyphics were used by ancient Egyptians for over 3,000 years but remained undeciphered until the Rosetta Stone was found in 1799.
