20 Mathematics Facts

← Back to Home

1. Zero was not used as a number in many ancient cultures — it was formally developed in India and later adopted by Arab mathematicians.
2. Pi (π) is an irrational number, meaning its decimal expansion never repeats and never ends — it has been calculated to over 100 trillion digits.
3. The Fibonacci sequence (0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8…) appears in patterns throughout nature, from flower petals to spiral shells.
4. A googol is 10 to the power of 100 — a number so large it exceeds the estimated number of atoms in the observable universe.
5. Prime numbers have fascinated mathematicians for millennia — the largest known prime as of recent years has tens of millions of digits.
6. Euler's identity (e^iπ + 1 = 0) is often called the most beautiful equation in mathematics, linking five fundamental constants.
7. The Pythagorean theorem has over 370 known proofs, making it one of the most proved theorems in mathematical history.
8. Infinity comes in different sizes — mathematician Georg Cantor proved that some infinities are larger than others.
9. A Möbius strip has only one surface and one edge — if you draw a line along its middle, you return to the start without lifting your pen.
10. Probability was largely developed in the 17th century as mathematicians tried to solve gambling problems for French aristocrats.
11. The four-colour theorem states that any map can be coloured using only four colours so no two adjacent regions share the same colour.
12. Negative numbers were once considered absurd by many European mathematicians and only became widely accepted in the 18th century.
13. Fractal geometry, developed largely by Benoit Mandelbrot, describes self-similar patterns that appear in coastlines, clouds, and snowflakes.
14. The number 1 is neither prime nor composite — it is classified as a unit in number theory.
15. Cryptography — the mathematics behind secure internet connections — relies heavily on the difficulty of factoring very large prime numbers.
16. Archimedes calculated a remarkably accurate approximation of pi around 250 BC using polygons inscribed in circles.
17. A perfect number equals the sum of its divisors (excluding itself) — 6 is perfect because 1 + 2 + 3 = 6.
18. Game theory, pioneered by John von Neumann, uses mathematics to model strategic decision-making in economics, politics, and biology.
19. The Königsberg Bridge Problem, solved by Euler in 1736, is considered the birth of graph theory and topology.
20. Andrew Wiles spent seven years in secret solving Fermat's Last Theorem in 1994, a problem that had stumped mathematicians for 358 years.
