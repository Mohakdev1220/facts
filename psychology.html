20 Psychology Facts

← Back to Home

1. The human brain can process an image seen for as little as 13 milliseconds, far faster than the blink of an eye.
2. Cognitive dissonance is the mental discomfort felt when a person holds two conflicting beliefs or acts against their values.
3. People are more likely to remember incomplete tasks than completed ones — a phenomenon called the Zeigarnik Effect.
4. The placebo effect demonstrates that believing a treatment works can produce real, measurable changes in the body.
5. Confirmation bias leads people to seek out and favor information that supports what they already believe.
6. Studies suggest that the average person has around 6,200 thoughts per day, many of which are repetitive.
7. Mirror neurons in the brain are thought to help people understand and imitate the actions and emotions of others.
8. The Dunning-Kruger Effect describes how people with limited knowledge in an area tend to overestimate their own competence.
9. Humans are wired for social connection — prolonged loneliness can have measurable negative effects on mental and physical health.
10. Classical conditioning, demonstrated by Pavlov, shows that behaviors and responses can be learned through repeated association.
11. The bystander effect means people are less likely to help in an emergency when other people are present.
12. Sleep is critical for memory consolidation — the brain replays and stores experiences during rest.
13. Emotional contagion means that moods and emotions can spread from person to person, much like a social virus.
14. People tend to make decisions based on emotion first and then justify those decisions with logic afterward.
15. The anchoring bias causes people to rely heavily on the first piece of information they encounter when making judgments.
16. Studies show that gratitude practices can measurably increase happiness and reduce symptoms of depression over time.
17. Intrinsic motivation — doing something for its own reward — tends to produce longer-lasting effort than external rewards.
18. The mere exposure effect means people tend to develop a preference for things simply because they are familiar with them.
19. Stress in short bursts can actually improve performance, but chronic stress damages memory, focus, and decision-making.
20. Self-compassion, treating yourself with the same kindness you would offer a friend, is strongly linked to resilience and wellbeing.
